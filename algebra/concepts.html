Linear Equations #linear_equation

A linear equation has variables only to the first power and graphs as a straight line.

  • Goal: isolate the variable using inverse operations.
  • Key skills: combining like terms, distributing, clearing fractions (carefully).
  • Typical forms: ax + b = c, or after simplifying.

Linear Inequalities #linear_inequality

Like linear equations, but the solution is a range of values.

  • Critical rule: when you multiply or divide by a negative number, flip the inequality sign.
  • Compound inequalities: solve each part while keeping the “and/or” meaning.
  • Graphing: open circle for < or >, closed circle for ≤ or ≥.

Absolute Value #absolute_value

Absolute value measures distance from 0: |x| ≥ 0.

  • Equations: |A| = k (with k ≥ 0) becomes A = k or A = -k.
  • Inequalities: translate distance ideas:
    • |A| < k means -k < A < k
    • |A| > k means A < -k or A > k
  • Graph: the parent y=|x| is V-shaped.

Systems (Equations & Matrices) #systems

A system is multiple equations with the same variables; the solution satisfies all of them.

  • Methods: substitution, elimination, graphing.
  • Interpretation: intersection point(s) of graphs.
  • Matrices (often Algebra 2/Precalc): represent systems and do operations like multiplication, determinant, and inverse (2×2 cases).
Back to top

Radicals & Exponent Rules #radicals

Radicals connect to rational exponents: a^(1/n) = √[n](a).

  • Exponent rules: product/quotient/power rules for simplifying expressions.
  • Simplifying radicals: pull out perfect squares (or perfect nth powers).
  • Important: √(x^2) = |x| because the principal square root is nonnegative.
Back to top

Radical Equations #radical_equations

Equations containing radicals (like square roots). You often eliminate the radical by squaring both sides.

  • Workflow: isolate the radical → square both sides → solve → check solutions.
  • Why check? squaring can create extraneous solutions that don’t satisfy the original equation.
  • Domain awareness: even before solving, the radicand must be valid (e.g., ≥ 0 for square roots of real numbers).

Complex Numbers #complex_numbers

Complex numbers extend real numbers using i, where i^2 = -1.

  • Standard form: a + bi.
  • Powers of i cycle: i, -1, -i, 1 (repeat every 4).
  • Quadratics: equations like x^2 + 4 = 0 have complex solutions x=±2i.

Polynomial Factoring #polynomials_factoring

Factoring rewrites a polynomial as a product of simpler expressions.

  • Common patterns: GCF, trinomials, difference of squares.
  • Cubes:
    • a^3 - b^3 = (a-b)(a^2 + ab + b^2)
    • a^3 + b^3 = (a+b)(a^2 - ab + b^2)
  • Why factor? solving equations, simplifying rational expressions, finding zeros.
Back to top

Quadratics #quadratics

A quadratic function is degree 2: ax^2 + bx + c. Its graph is a parabola.

  • Solving: factoring, completing the square, quadratic formula.
  • Vertex form: y = a(x-h)^2 + k shows vertex (h,k).
  • Discriminant: b^2 - 4ac tells number of real roots (2, 1, or 0).
  • Quadratic inequalities: solve by finding roots and using a sign chart/interval test.

Functions Basics #functions_basics

A function assigns exactly one output to each input. Notation: f(x).

  • Evaluate: substitute an input value into the rule.
  • Piecewise: different rules on different intervals.
  • Even/odd: even → y-axis symmetry; odd → origin symmetry.
  • Average rate of change: slope of secant line between two points.
Back to top

Domain & Range #functions_domain_range

Domain = allowed inputs. Range = possible outputs.

  • Rational expressions: denominator cannot be 0.
  • Even roots: radicand must be ≥ 0 for real-valued square roots.
  • Logarithms: input must be > 0.
  • Modeling: real-world constraints often restrict domain/range.
Back to top

Function Transformations #functions_transformations

Transformations move or reshape graphs from a parent function.

  • Shifts: f(x-h) right h; f(x)+k up k.
  • Stretches: af(x) vertical stretch if |a|>1, shrink if 0<|a|<1.
  • Reflections: -f(x) reflects over x-axis; f(-x) reflects over y-axis.

Composition & Inverses #functions_composition_inverse

Composition combines functions: (f∘g)(x)=f(g(x)).

  • Inverse: undoes a function; if invertible, f(f⁻¹(x))=x.
  • One-to-one test: horizontal line test on the graph.
  • Graph inverse: reflect across y=x.
  • Restricting domain: makes some functions (like x^2) invertible.

Polynomial Graphs #polynomials_graphs

Polynomial graphs are smooth and continuous. Key features come from degree and factors.

  • End behavior: controlled by degree (even/odd) and leading coefficient (positive/negative).
  • Zeros & multiplicity: odd multiplicity crosses the x-axis; even multiplicity touches/turns.
  • Turning points: at most degree − 1.
  • Max number of x-intercepts: at most the degree (counting real roots).
Back to top

Polynomial Division & Theorems #polynomials_division

Division helps rewrite polynomials and test factors efficiently.

  • Synthetic division: fast division by (x - c).
  • Remainder theorem: remainder when dividing by (x-c) equals p(c).
  • Factor theorem: (x-c) is a factor iff p(c)=0.
  • Long division: works for any divisor (including non-monic or higher degree).

Rational Expressions & Equations #rational_expressions

Rational expressions are fractions with polynomials.

  • Simplify: factor and cancel common factors (but keep domain restrictions).
  • Restrictions: original denominator ≠ 0, even if factors cancel later.
  • Rational equations: multiply by the LCD to clear denominators, then check restrictions.
  • Rational inequalities: use critical points (zeros and undefined points) and a sign chart.
Back to top

Rational Functions (Asymptotes & Holes) #rational_functions

Rational functions are graphs of rational expressions, with discontinuities.

  • Vertical asymptotes: denominator = 0 after simplifying (non-canceled factors).
  • Holes: canceled factors create removable discontinuities (a “missing point”).
  • Horizontal asymptotes: compare degrees:
    • deg top < deg bottom → y=0
    • deg top = deg bottom → ratio of leading coefficients
    • deg top > deg bottom → no horizontal (maybe slant/oblique)

Exponential Functions #exponentials

Exponential functions have the variable in the exponent, like a·b^x.

  • Growth vs decay: b>1 grows; 0<b<1 decays.
  • Interpretation: the base b is a multiplicative growth/decay factor per step.
  • Compare growth: exponentials eventually outgrow polynomials for large x.
Back to top

Logarithms #logarithms

Logs are inverses of exponentials: log_b(x)=y means b^y=x.

  • Domain: log input must be > 0.
  • Properties: product, quotient, power rules help expand/condense expressions.
  • Solving: rewrite as exponential form or use properties + exponent rules.
  • Change of base: log_b(a)=ln(a)/ln(b) (or common log).

Sequences & Series #sequences_series

A sequence is a list of terms; a series is the sum of terms.

  • Arithmetic: add a constant difference (linear pattern).
  • Geometric: multiply by a constant ratio (exponential pattern).
  • Explicit formulas: write a_n to compute the nth term directly.
  • Geometric sum: common Algebra 2 topic for finite sums.
Back to top

Modeling & Data #modeling

Modeling uses functions to describe real relationships and data trends.

  • Linear model: constant change per step (slope).
  • Exponential model: proportional change (percentage growth/decay).
  • Regression: finds a “best fit” function for data.
  • Outliers: unusual points can distort a model; interpret carefully.
  • Validation: models may fail outside the data range (extrapolation risk).
